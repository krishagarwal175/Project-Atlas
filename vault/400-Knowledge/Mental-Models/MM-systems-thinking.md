# 🧠 Systems Thinking

> See the business as stocks, flows, feedback loops, and delays — not a list of features. Leverage comes from changing loop structure, not pushing harder on symptoms.

## The idea
A **stock** accumulates (users, cash, trust, reputation). **Flows** change stocks (signups, churn, burn). **Feedback loops** connect them: reinforcing loops (growth compounds) and balancing loops (things that push back toward equilibrium). **Delays** hide cause from effect and cause overshoot. Most "obvious fixes" address a symptom while the loop regenerates the problem.

## When to reach for it
Growth, retention, and anything with compounding or self-correction. Essential for understanding [[MM-network-effects|network effects]] and [[MM-growth-loops|growth loops]], which are literally reinforcing loops.

## Why it works
Leverage points live in loop *structure* (add a reinforcing loop, remove a balancing one), not in effort. Delays explain why interventions seem not to work, then overshoot.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Over-modeling.** You don't need a full stock-and-flow diagram for every call — use it to find the one loop that matters.
- **Ignoring the balancing loops** that will fight your growth (e.g. leakage, saturation).

## Worked example
Marketplace: a reinforcing loop (more supply → more demand → more supply) is the prize; leakage is a balancing loop that drains it ([[P-marketplace-leakage-kills-unit-economics]]). Design to strengthen the first and dampen the second.

## Applies to Acredemia
The core loop: verified students → recruiter trust → recruiter demand → more students verify. Identify the balancing loops (leakage, trust decay) *before* pouring fuel in.
