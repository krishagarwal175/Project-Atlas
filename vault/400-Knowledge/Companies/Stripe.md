# 🏛 Stripe

> One-line thesis: **Win the decision-maker no one else was serving — the developer — by collapsing friction to near-zero.** Distribution *is* developer experience.

## Timeline (inflection points)
- 2010 — Patrick & John Collison found Stripe (YC 2010), frustrated by the friction of legacy gateways (PayPal, Authorize.net) that treated developers as an afterthought.
- 2011 — Public US launch (Sep 29) after a long private beta; ~$2M from Musk, Thiel, Sequoia, a16z.
- 2010s → — Expands from payments into a full financial-infrastructure platform.

## Key strategic decisions
- **Developer-first framing.** They recognized the *developer* had become the real buyer, yet no payments product treated them as the primary user. The famous premise: accept payments in **seven lines of code**.
- **Radical friction removal** as the entire wedge — a better *integration experience*, not a cheaper rate, was the differentiator.
- **Documentation & DX as marketing** — the product sold itself to the people who chose it.

## Decision patterns
- Serve the true decision-maker, not the nominal buyer ([[F-jtbd]]).
- Compete on *friction removed*, not price.
- Treat DX/onboarding as a distribution channel and a moat ([[F-porters-five-forces]]).

## Lessons for Acredemia
The recruiter (payer) may not be the *decision-maker* for trust — the candidate's *verified record* is what a recruiter actually "installs." Make the core action (get verified / trust a candidate) absurdly low-friction. First-mover on *experience* can beat first-mover on features.
