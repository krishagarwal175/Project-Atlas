# 🧠 The Marketplace Cold-Start Problem

> A two-sided marketplace is worthless to each side until the other side shows up — but neither shows up until it's valuable. Solving this chicken-and-egg is the central challenge of every marketplace.

## The idea
Below critical mass, [[MM-network-effects|network effects]] work against you. The proven escape routes: **(1) go narrow first** — win a single tight market (one city, one campus) to reach density before breadth (the "hard side / atomic network" approach); **(2) subsidize the harder side** to bootstrap supply; **(3) come to market with single-player utility** so the first side gets value even before the other exists; **(4) constrain quality** early rather than chasing volume.

## When to reach for it
Any two-sided launch or expansion sequencing decision. Directly relevant to [[Q-recruiters-first-or-students-first]].

## Why it works
Density in a narrow slice crosses the critical-mass threshold locally, igniting the reinforcing loop where broad-but-thin never would.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Scaling before density** (the Homejoy error — [[HomeJoy-marketplace-failure]]): expanding cities before any one retained.
- **Subsidizing the wrong (easy) side.**
- **Buying demand with discounts** that don't retain ([[Groupon-discount-growth]]).

## Applies to Acredemia
Which side is "harder"? Likely **verified-student supply** vs recruiter demand — pick a beachhead ([[Q-tier2-before-tier1]]), win density there, and give the first side standalone value before the other arrives. See [[Marketplace-Dynamics]].
