# 🧠 Base Rates & Bayesian Updating

> Start from the outside view (how often does this *usually* happen?), then update on evidence — don't reason from your specific story alone.

## The idea
Every belief has a **prior** (the base rate) and gets revised by **evidence** into a **posterior**. Bayes: `posterior ∝ prior × likelihood`. Founders systematically ignore base rates ("we're different") and over-weight vivid inside-view narratives. The base rate for a seed-stage startup reaching Series A, for a new feature moving retention, for a channel "working" — these are the anchor. Your evidence should *move* you off the anchor, not replace it.

## When to reach for it
Forecasting anything ("will this experiment succeed?"), evaluating a bold plan, or when a story feels too clean. It's the antidote to the [[MM-cognitive-biases|planning fallacy]].

## Why it works
Reference-class forecasting beats intuition empirically (Kahneman/Tversky). The base rate carries information your narrative doesn't.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Wrong reference class.** "Startups like us" is doing a lot of work — pick it honestly.
- **Base-rate neglect vs. genuine edge.** Sometimes you *are* different; the discipline is to *quantify* how much the evidence justifies moving, not to assume it.

## Worked example
"Our referral program will go viral." Base rate: most referral programs don't achieve self-sustaining virality. Evidence in the vault ([[T-two-sided-incentives-drive-referral-virality]], currently `emerging/contested`) modestly raises the posterior but the boundary condition (referred-user standalone utility) is unproven for us. Honest posterior: *plausible, not likely — run the cheap test.*

## Applies to Acredemia
Before any "this will work" claim, write the base rate first. The [[Vault-Governance|the epistemics engine]]/epistemics engine enforces the same discipline: confidence is *derived* from evidence, never asserted.
