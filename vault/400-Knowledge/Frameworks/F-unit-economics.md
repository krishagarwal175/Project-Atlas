# 🧰 Unit Economics

> Does one customer make money? **CAC** = cost to acquire one. **LTV** = gross margin they generate over their life. If LTV < CAC, growth *accelerates* losses — you're buying dollars for more than a dollar.

## What it is
The per-customer profitability that determines whether growth is a flywheel or a bonfire. Key numbers: **CAC**, **LTV** (gross-margin × lifetime, not revenue), the **LTV:CAC ratio** (≈3:1 is a common healthy heuristic), and **CAC payback period** (months to recoup CAC — runway-critical for a startup).

## When to use it
Before scaling any channel, changing pricing, or raising on a growth story. The number that separates real businesses from subsidized ones.

## How to run it
Compute CAC per channel (all-in). Compute LTV from **retention** and **gross margin** (revenue LTV lies). Check LTV:CAC and payback. Only pour money into a channel whose unit economics already work at small scale.

## Why it works
It makes the [[MM-second-order-thinking|second-order]] truth explicit: growth multiplies whatever the unit economics are. Positive → compounding; negative → faster death.

## Limitations / when NOT to use it
- **Early LTV is a guess** (little retention data); treat it as a range, not a point.
- **Blended CAC hides** a bad channel behind a good one — always segment.
- Ignoring payback period can be fatal even when LTV:CAC looks fine ([[MM-margin-of-safety]]).

## Applies to Acredemia
If students are free, recruiter-side revenue must carry CAC for *both* sides. Model recruiter LTV and payback before scaling GTM. This is the failure mode behind [[HomeJoy-marketplace-failure]] / [[Groupon-discount-growth]]: discount CAC, no retained LTV.
