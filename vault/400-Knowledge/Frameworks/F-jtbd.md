# 🧰 Jobs To Be Done

> People don't buy products; they "hire" them to make progress in a situation. Understand the *job*, and the product, competitor set, and positioning follow.

## What it is
A lens that defines the market by the customer's *progress* ("get a trustworthy signal of my ability to a recruiter") rather than by demographics or product category. The famous framing: "the customer hired a milkshake for the job of making a boring commute bearable." Competitors are anything else that does the job — often not the products you think.

## When to use it
Discovery, positioning, and deciding what to build. Especially when retention is weak (you may be built for the wrong job) or the competitor set is unclear.

## How to run it
1. Find the situation that triggers the job. 2. Describe the desired progress + the anxieties/habits blocking it. 3. Identify what they "hire" today (including doing nothing). 4. Design to do the job better and reduce the switching anxiety.

## Why it works
Jobs are stable while products churn. Anchoring on the job prevents feature-chasing and reveals the true competition.

## Limitations / when NOT to use it
- Not a substitute for quantitative validation ([[F-lean-startup]]).
- Over-broad jobs ("be successful") are useless; keep the situation specific.

## Applies to Acredemia
Student's job: "prove my ability so a recruiter trusts me without a background-check tax." Recruiter's job: "find candidates I can trust quickly." Two jobs, two sides — see [[User-Personas]] and [[Q-recruiters-first-or-students-first]].
