# 🧠 Cognitive Biases — the founder's shortlist

> Predictable errors in judgment. You can't eliminate them, but naming them lets you build countermeasures into the *process*. Atlas's whole design is a bias-countermeasure.

## The shortlist that costs founders the most
- **Confirmation bias** — seeking evidence that supports what you already want. *Countermeasure:* the [[Vault-Governance|Contradiction Engine]] — every theory must survive a search for evidence *against* it before its confidence can rise.
- **Planning fallacy** — systematic optimism about time/cost/success. *Countermeasure:* [[MM-base-rates]] (reference-class forecasting), pre-mortems ([[MM-inversion]]).
- **Sunk-cost fallacy** — throwing good money after bad because you've already invested. *Countermeasure:* judge decisions on *forward* EV; the decision log separates [[MM-decision-quality-vs-outcome|quality from outcome]].
- **Survivorship bias** — learning only from winners (every "how X succeeded" story). *Countermeasure:* deliberately curate failures ([[HomeJoy-marketplace-failure]], [[Groupon-discount-growth]]).
- **Narrative fallacy** — a clean story feels true. *Countermeasure:* demand provenance; the [[Vault-Governance|the governance bot]] flags claims without evidence.
- **Overconfidence** — 90%-sure predictions are right ~70% of the time. *Countermeasure:* [[MM-calibration]].
- **Anchoring & availability** — over-weighting the first number or the most vivid recent case.

## Why the "shortlist" approach
You can't hold 180 biases in mind. Hold six, and route around them structurally rather than relying on willpower.

## Applies to Acredemia
Atlas is, deliberately, an anti-bias operating system: derived confidence (not asserted), forced contradiction searches, curated failures, quality≠outcome. Using it *is* the countermeasure.
