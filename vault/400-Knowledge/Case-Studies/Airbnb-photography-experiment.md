# 📄 Airbnb — The Photography Experiment

## Problem
Early 2009: traffic existed but bookings didn't. Revenue was flat and the company was near-death. The founders couldn't see *why* visitors weren't converting.

## Strategy
Following Paul Graham's advice to **"do things that don't scale,"** they stopped looking for a scalable fix and looked at the actual listings. The insight: the host-supplied **photos were terrible** — and in a stranger-to-stranger marketplace, the photo *is* the trust signal that drives the booking decision.

## Execution
The founders travelled to New York, rented a professional camera, and went **door-to-door re-shooting listings** themselves — a completely un-scalable, manual intervention. No product change, no algorithm.

## Outcome (with sources)
Weekly revenue roughly **doubled** shortly after. The effect was later validated at scale: a Carnegie Mellon study found professional photography raised listing revenue by ~**$2,455/year** on average, with travellers ~**2.5× more likely to book** well-photographed listings. Airbnb then **systematized** the hack into a free professional-photography program.

## Why it worked
- The bottleneck was **trust/quality at the listing level**, invisible in aggregate metrics.
- Manual work surfaced the real lever fast; only *then* was it worth automating.
- In marketplaces, conversion is gated by trust signals more than by features.

## Transferability to Acredemia
Directly analogous: the **quality of the verified profile** is Acredemia's "photo" — the trust artifact that gates recruiter conversion. Early on, do the un-scalable thing (hand-verify, hand-craft great profiles, hand-hold recruiters) to learn what actually builds trust, *then* systematize.
