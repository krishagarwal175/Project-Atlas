# 🏛 Amazon

> One-line thesis: **Design one reinforcing loop, then feed it relentlessly with long-term, customer-obsessed bets that look irrational quarter-to-quarter.**

## Timeline (inflection points)
- ~2000–2001 — Bezos sketches the **flywheel** ("virtuous cycle") — reportedly on a napkin, drawing on a Jim Collins conversation.
- Feb 2005 — **Amazon Prime** launches: unlimited two-day shipping for **$79/yr** — expensive and bold for its time; a bet that convenience would lift long-term purchasing.
- 2006+ — AWS turns internal infrastructure into a new flywheel of its own.

## The flywheel (why it compounds)
Lower prices → more customers → more third-party sellers → wider selection + scale → lower cost structure → still lower prices. A classic reinforcing loop ([[MM-systems-thinking]]); every part feeds the next, so effort compounds instead of dissipating.

## Key strategic decisions
- **Prime as a loyalty loop** — a subscription that changes buying behavior and raises switching costs; short-term costly, long-term compounding.
- **Long-term orientation** — willingness to depress near-term profit for durable advantage.
- **Customer obsession as the flywheel's input.**

## Decision patterns
- Find the loop, then optimize the whole loop — not isolated metrics ([[MM-second-order-thinking]]).
- Make bold, expensive customer-experience bets ([[MM-expected-value]] with a long horizon).

## Lessons for Acredemia
Name Acredemia's flywheel explicitly (verified students → recruiter trust → demand → more verification) and judge every initiative by whether it *feeds the loop*. Some trust-building investments will look expensive short-term and be right long-term.
