# 🧠 Inversion

> "Invert, always invert." Instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to guarantee failure — then avoid that. Solving the negative is often easier and more reliable than solving the positive.

## The idea
For many problems, the failure modes are clearer than the success recipe. You may not know how to make Acredemia win, but you can enumerate what would kill it: losing student trust, running out of runway, building for a segment that won't pay, a competitor owning verification. Avoiding the known killers is a more robust strategy than chasing an uncertain optimum.

## When to reach for it
Risk analysis, pre-mortems, strategy when the path to success is fuzzy but the failure modes are legible, and designing guardrails.

## Why it works
Avoiding stupidity is easier than seeking brilliance (Munger). Failure modes are often fewer and more concrete than success paths, and negative constraints compound into a defensible position.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Pure defense doesn't win.** Inversion bounds the downside; you still need an offensive thesis.
- **Over-indexing on rare catastrophes** at the cost of momentum.

## Worked example
This is exactly what [[DNA-Non-Negotiables]] encodes — inversion applied to Acredemia's identity: "never fake verification, never sell student data, never paywall basic truth." Those are pre-committed *avoid-failure* rules, enforced as a hard guardrail by the [[F-MCDA-weighted-sum|Decision Engine]].

## Applies to Acredemia
Run a **pre-mortem** before big bets: "It's a year later and this failed — why?" Feed the answers into the decision's *"what would change my mind"* and its experiment's failure conditions.
