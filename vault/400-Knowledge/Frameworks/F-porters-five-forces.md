# 🧰 Porter's Five Forces

> Industry profitability is set by five structural forces. Where you sit relative to them determines whether you can earn durable margins — or get competed to zero.

## What it is
The five: **(1) Rivalry** among existing players, **(2) Threat of new entrants** (barriers to entry), **(3) Supplier power**, **(4) Buyer power**, **(5) Threat of substitutes**. Strong forces compress profits; weak forces (high barriers, low buyer/supplier power, few substitutes) allow durable advantage.

## When to use it
Assessing a market's attractiveness, planning a moat, anticipating competitive response. Complements [[MM-scenario-planning]] for "what if a competitor moves."

## How to run it
Rate each force high/med/low with the reasoning, then ask: which force most threatens us, and what structural move (network effects, switching costs, exclusive supply) weakens it?

## Why it works
It shifts attention from *your* company to the *structure* of the game — where sustainable advantage actually comes from.

## Limitations / when NOT to use it
- Static snapshot; industries with fast platform dynamics need [[MM-systems-thinking]] and network-effects analysis layered on.
- Underweights complementors and ecosystems.
- Descriptive, not prescriptive — tells you where you stand, not what to do.

## Applies to Acredemia
Buyer power (recruiters), substitutes (LinkedIn, background-check vendors, doing nothing), entrant threat (an incumbent adds verification). The intended moat: **trust + two-sided network effects** ([[MM-network-effects]]) that raise switching costs. See [[Competitors]].
