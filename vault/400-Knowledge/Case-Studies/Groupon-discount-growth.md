# 📄 Groupon — Discount-Driven Growth

## Problem
Groupon's daily-deals marketplace needed rapid two-sided growth: consumers on one side, local merchants on the other.

## Strategy
Aggressive deep-discount promotions to pull in consumers fast, funded by merchants offering steep one-time deals in exchange for exposure.

## Execution
Massive email-driven deal blasts; enormous sales force signing local merchants; grew explosively into a ~$13B IPO in 2011.

## Outcome (metrics)
Explosive early growth, but a long decline followed: many merchants found deals unprofitable and reported that deal-seekers rarely returned at full price; consumers suffered "deal fatigue"; the stock fell dramatically over the following years. A canonical cautionary tale, not an outright shutdown — hence `outcome: mixed`.

## Why it underperformed
- **Discount-acquired demand didn't retain** — consumers optimized for the discount, not the merchant relationship.
- Merchants churned when deals were unprofitable and brought no repeat customers.
- Growth was promotion-driven, not loyalty-driven — the same failure mode as [[HomeJoy-marketplace-failure]].

## Transferability to Acredemia
Reinforces the boundary condition that **discount/incentive-driven acquisition ≠ retained growth**. If Acredemia ever subsidizes one side of the market to grow, this is the failure mode to design against. Feeds [[P-marketplace-leakage-kills-unit-economics]].
