# 🧠 Second-Order Thinking

> "And then what?" Trace the consequences of the consequences. First-order effects are obvious and often misleading; second- and third-order effects are where the real outcome lives.

## The idea
Every action ripples. Cutting price (1st order: more signups) → (2nd order: lower-quality, discount-seeking users who don't retain) → (3rd order: skewed metrics, wrong product signals). The discipline is to ask "and then what?" at least twice before committing, and to watch for effects that *reverse* the first-order intent.

## When to reach for it
Any decision that changes incentives, pricing, or who you attract; anything involving other agents who will react (competitors, users, recruiters).

## Why it works
Systems have feedback loops and delays. First-order reasoning treats the world as static; second-order reasoning models it as reactive. Most strategic mistakes are correct first-order calls with unconsidered second-order costs.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Analysis paralysis / infinite regress.** Stop when further orders are speculative. Two to three orders is usually enough.
- **Using it to justify inaction.** Every option has second-order risks; compare them, don't just fear them.

## Worked example
Homejoy & Groupon ([[P-marketplace-leakage-kills-unit-economics]]): first-order "discounts drive growth" → second-order "discount-acquired demand doesn't retain and leaks off-platform" → collapse. The pattern is a second-order failure that first-order growth metrics hid.

## Applies to Acredemia
Free student verification (1st order: adoption) → (2nd order: trust flywheel, but who funds it?) → tie to [[Q-should-verification-stay-free]]. Any incentive we add, trace to who it *actually* attracts.
