# 🏛 Zerodha

> One-line thesis: **A hard constraint (never raise VC) forced profitability, discipline, and trust — and still produced India's largest broker.** Bootstrapping as strategy, not limitation. (Especially relevant to an Indian founder.)

## Timeline (inflection points)
- Aug 2010 — Nithin & Nikhil Kamath found Zerodha with a rule: **never raise external capital**; ~₹2 crore of personal/friend seed, six employees.
- 2010s — Flat **₹20-per-trade** pricing disrupts the standard ~0.3%-of-turnover commission model.
- 2020s — Grows to India's **largest retail broker** (millions of active clients); FY22 reportedly **₹4,964 cr revenue / ₹2,094 cr profit** — highly profitable, still no VC.

## Key strategic decisions
- **Bootstrapping as a forcing function.** No VC meant every rupee had to be earned — profitability from early, no growth-at-all-costs.
- **Radical pricing disruption** (flat fee vs. % commission) — a [[MM-first-principles|first-principles]] rethink of what a trade should cost.
- **Trust + education** (Varsity, transparent product) as retention, not paid acquisition.
- Grew largely by **word-of-mouth / product**, not ad spend ([[MM-growth-loops]]).

## Decision patterns
- Constraints breed discipline; profitability is a strategy, not an afterthought ([[MM-margin-of-safety]]).
- Win trust in a low-trust category, then let it compound.

## Lessons for Acredemia
Bootstrapping discipline and *trust as the moat* map directly onto Acredemia (Indian market, trust-centric, [[DNA-Non-Negotiables]]). Pricing can be a first-principles wedge. You don't need VC to build a category leader — you need retained, profitable unit economics ([[F-unit-economics]]).
