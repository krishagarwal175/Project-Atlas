# 🧠 Decision Quality vs. Outcome

> A good decision can have a bad outcome; a bad decision can have a good outcome. Judging a decision by its result — "resulting" — is one of the most expensive errors a founder makes.

## The idea
Decision **quality** is about the *process* at the time: available evidence, reasoning, alternatives explored, assumptions made explicit, risk awareness. **Outcome** is what reality delivered, which includes luck. Because outcomes are visible and process is not, we lazily infer quality from outcome — punishing good bets that lost and praising reckless bets that won. Over time this destroys good judgment.

## When to reach for it
Every retrospective. Every "that worked, do it again" or "that failed, never again."

## Why it works
In any probabilistic domain, only process is controllable and repeatable. Optimize process and outcomes follow *in aggregate* (see [[MM-expected-value]]); optimize on single outcomes and you learn superstitions.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Excuse-making.** "Good decision, bad luck" can hide a genuinely bad process. Separate honestly.
- Requires **calibration** ([[MM-calibration]]) to know whether outcomes are beating or lagging your process quality.

## Worked example
Atlas enforces this structurally: every decision note scores **quality at decision time** and records **outcome at retrospective** in *separate* sections that must never be merged ([[Vault-Governance]]). The calibration strip compares them over time.

## Applies to Acredemia
When reviewing a bet, grade the decision *and* the result independently. Reward good process even when the result disappoints — that's how judgment compounds.
