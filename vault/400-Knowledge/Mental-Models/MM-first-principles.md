# 🧠 First-Principles Thinking

> Reason up from what must be true, not sideways from what others do. Strip an assumption to its irreducible facts, then rebuild.

## The idea
Most reasoning is by analogy ("competitors charge ₹X, so we should too"). Analogy carries hidden, possibly-wrong assumptions. First-principles thinking asks: what do we *actually know* to be true, and what follows *only* from that? It's slower and more expensive, so reserve it for the few decisions where the conventional wisdom might be wrong and being right is worth a lot.

## When to reach for it
Pricing, core product bets, and anywhere "that's how it's done" is the main justification. It's the source of genuine differentiation — copied assumptions produce copied companies.

## Why it works
Analogies inherit the constraints of their source context, which may not apply to yours. Rebuilding from facts finds options the herd can't see.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Expensive.** Don't re-derive everything; most decisions should use base rates and analogy. Spend first-principles effort on the vital few.
- **Ignoring hard-won convention.** Sometimes "how it's done" encodes real lessons — check whether the constraint still holds before discarding it.
