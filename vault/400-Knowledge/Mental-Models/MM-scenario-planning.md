# 🧠 Scenario Planning

> When you can't forecast a single future, plan for a *set* of plausible ones. Build a few coherent scenarios, then choose actions that are robust across most of them.

## The idea
Instead of betting on one prediction, identify the 2–4 futures that matter (e.g. "recruiters pay readily" vs "recruiters resist paying"; "a big incumbent enters" vs "not"). For each, ask what you'd do. Then prefer **robust** moves (good across scenarios) and **cheap options** (small bets that pay off big in one scenario) over all-in commitments to a single forecast.

## When to reach for it
Irreducible uncertainty: fundraising timing, competitive response, regulatory shifts, "which segment first." Complements [[MM-expected-value]] (use EV when you can estimate p; scenarios when you can't).

## Why it works
It converts "I don't know the future" from paralysis into structured preparation, and reveals no-regret moves you'd otherwise miss. It also pre-loads your reactions so you act fast when a scenario starts materializing.

## Failure modes / where it misleads
- **Too many scenarios** → mush. Keep to 2–4 distinct ones.
- **False robustness** — labeling a bet "robust" to avoid committing. Be honest about which scenario you're actually betting on.

## Worked example
[[Q-recruiters-first-or-students-first]] is a scenario problem: sequence the side that's robust to the cold-start failing, and keep the other side a cheap option. Pair with [[MM-inversion]] pre-mortems for each scenario.

## Applies to Acredemia
Maintain 2–3 live scenarios for the next 12 months (funding, competition, WTP). Tag decisions with which scenario they assume; when signals shift (the Signals workspace), revisit.
